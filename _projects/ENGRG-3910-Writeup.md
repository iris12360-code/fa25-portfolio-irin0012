People were always counting down weeks in April, while it was that afternoon after the ceremony we knew it ended. There are so many lessons learned along the way and I want to record them down from this program. 


There are ethical codes that we as engineers especially need to hold. I learned some of them as standards written in NSPE which have been verified and testified to not having conflict with each other. However, there are cases that the standard conflicts with what people personally pursue or value. For example, passing a road when the red light on may force penalties, while I would still cross it when no cars are on the road. I realize this would actually cause problems when there were actually cars in my blind spot or I am paying less attention to the road. I started to reflect on my ethical code: I really tend to become the type of person who will only learn the lesson if they have taken the responsibility for this. However, this time human lives and some responsibilities for faults in engineering careers are probably way more heavy than I can hold.  
Therefore, the way I approach to solve my “unethical” problem is to imagine a third eye— the sky, the god view. To make the decisions, test my intended choice with this third eye as the fair judge, and I would feel no guilt if it passed. However, there is also another way around that is that I am alone when people all cross the red light but I don’t. This thing is small so I don’t feel pressured to follow the group, but what if it comes to more people’s interests? 


Besides thinking about ethical codes, I also have lessons about following the lead. Sometimes in our weekly meetings with my group mates in the Certificate program, I noticed myself comfortable as a follower giving feedback to others’ ideas and taking actions to do work as people proposed. I somehow lacked the initiative to propose plans, to share my ideas at the beginning. However, it is not all the cases: I noticed it normally happens when I am not motivated in that topic. This actually gives me some good news that once I aligned with the ideas, I would not give up having my voice heard. When it comes to our Change Game, my hesitation or no-action state gets exaggerated. Because I was told clearly what to do to secure my money, while the other side of the choice is the uncertainty of losing my money. I seriously felt the words to speak up to break the barrier of isolation between two sides of the room was so close to my mouth; but I was so used to not speaking up so no words, or specifically public suggestions, were said by me at that time. Now, that moment actually hit me standing out to me from that session as that was a chance that I can comfortably do it in front of a group of people I trust, and I missed it. From that, I really think about how to decide when to speak up and become that leading voice. My current conclusion is to raise my hand first when something comes up like the rush to speak up before my “rational thinking” comes to convince me not to do that, which makes me have to say something as indication of speaking up has already been given out. This session definitely has me reflect about my considerations and decisions to follow the lead or lead the thing more. 


Finally I want to talk about some thoughts I learned from giving and receiving feedback. In the feedback I received, I can see another external perspective of my behavior and presence. It is very useful and alert for me as I realized something I perceived is different from what other people perceive. At a time when I received feedback from my systemizers, I got to know I have been actually often late for the meeting as it would be weird to bring it up right in the face I guess. Also at this point, I noticed I did not react aggressively internally towards the critical feedback, because I am prepared to hear some honest voices. This gives me insights about giving feedback to others: don’t throw a bomb to others when they are not prepared for it, or it may cause radical reactions so that feedback won’t open up the conversation and instead may shift the point to stronger emotions or confrontation. Also I found out the process of giving and receiving feedback is such a nice communication loop to dissolve misunderstandings. We can talk about every piece of them in details: is this true? Is there assumption in the feedback? What should I do to deal with some of the true problems in the critical feedback? Actions to do become more and more clear in these questions. Besides, it opens up space for future conversations. We can get back to these things anther time in the future to check on ourselves. I really appreciate this progressive idea of giving and receiving feedback. Not only critical feedback, positive feedbacks can also get me to recognize others’ effort and things to learn from them, and identify the appreciation and recognization from others. 


I feel geniuely disbelief that leadership Certificate program has already come to the end at this time. However, I do believe the lessons I took away from here and friendship as well as connections I made here will carry me along the way from now to the future.
